The Canon cameras we use have a resolution of 6,720 x 4,480 pixels - that's over 30 megapixels! But what does that really mean? Apple's iPhone 14 Pro is advertised as having 48 megapixel capability, so is that better?

Nope.

We're actually comparing apples with pears here. Check out this table:<br>

| Sensor Size | Width (mm) | Height (mm) | Area (mm<sup>2</sup>)  |
| -------- | -------- | -------- | -------- |
| **iPhone 14 Pro** | 7.6      | 5.7    | 43 |
| **Canon Full-frame** | 36      | 24     | 864 |

The iPhone's sensor (the bit that 'sees' and records the digital image) is tiny by comparison with the full-frame sensors we use, so is nowhere near as accurate.

The sensor dictates the quality of the images a camera can produce - the larger the sensor, the higher the image quality. A bigger image sensor has bigger pixels, meaning improved low-light performance, reduced noise, good dynamic range, and the ability to record more information.

Add to that the difference in lenses, and the gulf in quality just gets bigger. Our professional lenses are calibrated to deliver accurate colour and sharp images, without aberration or distortion. The iPhone relies on software to compensate for its fixed focal length lenses, which can cause issues, and if you zoom into the photos you'll see pixellation much earlier - you'll certainly struggle to blow them up to poster-size.

Is an iPhone "bad"? No, it's remarkable, but it doesn't afford the level of control or quality that we can achieve with our equipment.

The photos you see on our site are converted to JPG and reduced in size by 50%, so they're 3,360 x 2,240 pixels, and we use compression to 80% quality, in order to reduce file sizes - somewhere between 1-3Mb - the original 'RAW' images that we capture in camera are usually around 40-50Mb!

(as an aside, the photos embedded in our blog posts are all reduced to 800 pixels wide, so they're tiny!)

Your final edited images can be delivered in any format - TIFF is the best quality, as it's 'lossless' - and can easily be blown up to larger sizes without compromising quality - just bear in mind that these can be 140Mb+ per photo!

More commonly, you'll want JPG. Although it's 'lossy', the images we deliver to you will be 1st-generation files, so won't be visibly degraded - these will generally be more manageable, as they'll be roughly 6-12Mb.

The bottom line is that we can supply your images to whatever level of quality, size and format you need. You just have to tell us.
