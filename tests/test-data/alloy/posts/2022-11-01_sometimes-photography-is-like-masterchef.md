You know on Masterchef, when a contestant says that instead of 'Velociraptor Kidneys, cooked three ways, served with Edible Strontium-90, in a Dodo Tears Jus', they're going to deliver a dish that - on the face of it, at least - is simple; then Gregg gurns, whilst bellowing that "there's nowhere to hide with this plate of food"?

Well, some photos are like that.

Does it mean that they're 'point-and-shoot'? That *anyone* can deliver them? That "I could take that on my iPhone"?

Nope. Not by a long shot.

Take this shot of an Yves St Laurent lipstick. It's actually *really* difficult to properly depict metallic surfaces in a photograph, so an in-depth understanding of lighting and lighting modifiers is critical.

With metallic surfaces being so reflective, it's also difficult to avoid *ugly* reflections, so knowledge of angles of incidence and reflection are crucial.

It's also *really* difficult to photograph small subjects so close-up, making sure the entire thing is perfectly in focus, so focus-stacking is needed.

That's just what's needed to avoid the pitfalls. An immense amount of care is needed, to ensure that the product is lit with a beautiful graduated light, whilst also having a well-defined edge light. Whilst keeping colours true to reality.

And all whilst making sure that the composition is compelling and classy - properly conveying brand values.

![](https://ktbb.co.uk/image-uploads/YSL_Lipstick-800.jpg)

Gregg is absolutely correct - when you deliver a "simple" plate of food, there's nowhere to hide - but as long as it's been prepared with understanding, care and attention-to-detail, and all the flavours complement each other so that it tastes delicious, what's not to love?
